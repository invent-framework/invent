# Invent Framework - Design Brief

## Purpose of This Document

This brief is for a designer (or design team) commissioned to create a
logo and visual identity for the Invent Framework. It provides the
philosophical context, practical constraints, and creative direction
needed to produce work that embodies the project's values.

A companion document, *Invent Framework - Brand Articulation*, provides
the full brand statement. This brief assumes familiarity with that
document and focuses on actionable design guidance.

---

## 1. What Is Invent?

Invent is an open-source Python-in-the-browser app framework. It allows
people - including beginners, non-coders working with AI, educators,
hobbyists, knowledge workers and developers new to GUI work - to easily
build applications in Python that run directly in a web browser.

Invent is an independent open source project. The visual identity must
stand entirely on its own.

## 2. The Challenge

**Invent's philosophy is that the framework should disappear.** Its
success is measured by how quickly the user forgets about it and becomes
absorbed in their own creative flow.

The visual identity must embody this principle. It needs to be:

- **Present enough to be recognised** - a clear, memorable mark.
- **Quiet enough to recede** - it should not dominate any context in which
  it appears.
- **Meaningful on inspection** - rewarding to those who look closely,
  but never demanding that attention.

This is the central tension the design must resolve: *how do you create
a memorable identity for something whose purpose is to become invisible?*

## 3. The Maker's Mark Concept

Upon reflection, the right territory for this identity is the **maker's
mark** or **hallmark** - not the billboard, not the corporate symbol.

A maker's mark is:

- Stamped into the base of a handmade bowl, not printed on the box.
- Evidence of care and provenance.
- Discovered by the person who looks, not thrust upon the person who
  glances.
- Historically rooted in craft traditions (Arts and Crafts movement,
  guild marks, potters' stamps).

The Invent mark should feel like it *belongs* on the things people make
with it. Not imposed, but part of the work's identity. A quiet
declaration: "this was made with care, using Invent."

### The Process of Disappearing

An important nuance: the mark should acknowledge the *process* of
receding. It must be fully visible and noticed first - its disappearance
into the background should feel deliberate and graceful, not accidental.
Consider a watermark: fully visible on a blank page, invisible once the
page is full of the user's own work.

This "two-state" quality (present / receded) could inform:

- How the logo appears in documentation versus in a running app.
- Colour and opacity choices.
- Animated transitions (if appropriate for digital contexts).

### The Two Phrases

The mark has two verbal modes, each paired with the logo:

- **"Go Invent."** - used on the project's own materials: the website,
  documentation, GitHub, conference slides, community communications.
  This is the invitation. It faces outward, toward the person who
  hasn't started yet. It is warm, encouraging, and quietly confident -
  a benediction, not a command. (It also carries the overtone of team
  encouragement: "Go team!" — making the community feel like a
  collective endeavour.)

- **"Invented!"** - used as a maker's mark in apps built with the
  framework. This is the stamp of provenance. It faces backward, toward
  the thing that has been made. It is a quiet declaration of completion
  and pride: *this was made with care, using Invent.*

The two phrases mirror the framework's own lifecycle: Invent arrives,
encourages ("Go Invent."), then recedes into a quiet hallmark on the
finished work ("Invented!").

## 4. The Word "Invent"

The name is settled and offers rich material:

- **It is a noun, a verb, and an imperative.** "Invent" names the
  framework, describes what you do with it, and encourages you to act.
  "Go Invent!" is a complete sentence.
- **Etymology:** From Latin *invenire* — "to come upon," "to discover."
  Not to manufacture or fabricate, but to find something that was already
  latent. This aligns with the brand philosophy: Invent helps you
  discover through an act of digital creation.
- **Hidden structure:** The word contains "I" (the individual), "in"
  (entry, inclusion), and "vent" (to give voice to, to express). These
  are not accidents and could be explored visually.
- **Letterform qualities:** The vertical rhythm of I-n-v-e-n-t, the
  near-symmetry of the two n's, and the geometry of v as a pivot point
  are all worth exploring.
- **Negative space:** Given the disappearing-framework philosophy,
  what is *implied* or *discovered between* the letters may be more
  important than what is explicitly drawn. The FedEx arrow (the hidden
  forward-pointing arrow between the E and x) and the Amazon A-to-Z
  smile are interesting precedents - not for their style, but for
  their principle: a simple mark with a hidden reward for the attentive
  viewer.

## 5. Visual Direction

### Philosophy, Not Ornament

The brand draws philosophical inspiration from the **Arts and Crafts
movement** (William Morris, the guild tradition): dignity of the maker,
honest materials, beauty as a democratic right, rejection of soulless
industrial production.

However, the *visual* expression should be **modern and restrained** -
not ornamental, not historicist. Take the values of Morris; express them
with the economy of Dieter Rams.

### Personality Keywords

These words should guide visual choices:

- Confidently open - approachable and engaging, yet operating at a
  level of quiet excellence that needs no advertisement.
- Seriously playful - sophisticated, generative play with freedom and
  full concentration. Not childish.
- Quietly ambitious - extraordinary craft in service of something
  beyond itself.
- Timeless and deep - ancient archetypes rediscovered in a digital
  realm.

### What It Should Feel Like

> Using Invent should feel like it's always supposed to be this way.
> Ancient archetypes rediscovered in a digital realm, experienced as
> pure inevitability — as if Invent was always there, waiting to be
> unlocked.

The visual identity should evoke this same quality. Not "new and
exciting." Not "retro and familiar." Something that feels as though it
was always there, waiting to be noticed, discovered or uncovered.

## 6. Values to Embody

Invent's values are expressed as paradoxical pairs - the first term
privileged, the second retained as an essential counterweight. This
tension drives invention, and it should inform design decisions:

- **Humanity over technology** - the mark should feel human, not
  machine-generated.
- **Play over rules** - within a recognisable formal structure.
- **Empowerment over process** - the identity should feel enabling,
  not institutional.
- **Dignity over direction** - respectful, not prescriptive.
- **Honesty over hiding** - no visual pretence or gratuitous
  embellishment.
- **Expression over explaining** - show, don't tell.

## 7. Colour

### Guiding Principle

Invent's colour palette must be **unobtrusive** — it should work
alongside the user's own colour choices, not compete with them.

### Direction

- Prefer **natural tones** over synthetic ones. Authentic, not
  artificial. Think the sky, flesh, earth, stone, ink, parchment - not
  corporate blue, not neon, not plastic.
- Avoid saturated, "branded" colours that insist on attention. The
  Anaconda brand green is cited as an explicit anti-reference: an
  unnatural, synthetic green.
- Consider a palette that works in **two states**: a full-colour
  "present" state and a muted or monochrome "receded" state, reflecting
  the disappearing-framework concept.
- The mark must work beautifully in **pure black on white** and **pure
  white on black**. Colour should be an enhancement, not a dependency.

### Accessibility

Whichever palette is chosen:

- Ensure sufficient contrast ratios (WCAG AA minimum, AAA preferred).
- Test for colour-blind accessibility (protanopia, deuteranopia,
  tritanopia).
- The mark must be fully legible and recognisable without colour.

## 8. Typography

No specific typeface is mandated, but the following preferences apply:

- **Do not mix serif and sans-serif.** Choose one family.
- Prefer a **free-to-use, modern, elegant, and accessible** typeface.
- The typeface should feel **timeless rather than trendy** - avoid
  anything that will date within five years.
- If the logo includes a wordmark, it should have the quality of
  something handset with care, not generated by default.
- A **fixed-width companion font** will be needed for code contexts.
  This should be equally considered and elegant.

## 9. Anti-References

The following are directions to actively avoid:

| Avoid | Why |
|---|---|
| Synthetic / neon colours | Inauthentic. Invent values honest, natural materials. |
| Google-style primary-colour playfulness | Infantile. Invent's play is sophisticated and serious. |
| Billboard word-brands (Netflix, Meta) | Invent does not demand attention. It earns it quietly. |
| Ornamental / historicist styling | Arts and Crafts philosophy, not Arts and Crafts aesthetics. |
| "Tech startup" aesthetics (gradients, rounded sans-serifs, generic icon + wordmark) | Generic. Invent should feel confidently unique in the framework ecosystem. |
| Overly clever or puzzle-like logos | The hidden meaning should be a quiet reward, not a riddle. |
| Anything that says "look how powerful/fast/scalable this is" | That is not what Invent is about. Invent's focus is expressing through code. |

## 10. Applications

The logo and identity will need to work across these contexts:

### Primary

- **Homepage / Documentation website** - the most important context.
  The mark sits alongside technical content and should complement, not
  compete.
- **GitHub repository** - README header, social preview image.
- **PyPI listing** - small square icon.

### Secondary

- **Conference slides** - the mark may appear on presentation title
  slides or footers.
- **Stickers and T-shirts** - community swag for conferences and
  meetups.
- **"Invented!" badge** - a small, unobtrusive mark that could
  appear in apps built with the framework (this is the maker's mark
  use case - see *The Two Phrases* below).

### Practical Constraints

- Must be legible at very small sizes (16×16 favicon, PyPI icon).
- Must work in monochrome (single colour on any background).
- Must work on both light and dark backgrounds.
- Should have a compact icon/mark variant and a full wordmark variant.
- No dependency on fine detail that breaks at low resolution.

## 11. Deliverables Requested

1. **Primary logo** - the full mark (icon + wordmark if applicable).
2. **Icon variant** - a compact mark for small contexts (favicons,
   avatars, badges).
3. **"Invented!" badge** - for use in apps built with the framework.
4. **"Go Invent." lockup** - logo + phrase for project-facing materials.
5. **Colour palette** - primary and secondary colours with hex values,
   including light/dark mode variants and a "receded" state.
6. **Typography recommendation** - primary typeface and fixed-width
   companion, with fallback stacks.
7. **Usage guidelines** - minimum sizes, clear space, colour
   backgrounds, what not to do.
8. **Source files** - vector format (SVG), plus PNG exports at
   standard sizes.

## 12. Process and Collaboration

The designer is encouraged to:

- Read the companion *Brand Articulation* document in full before
  beginning visual exploration.
- Present initial concepts as rough explorations, not polished
  proposals. We value honest work-in-progress over premature polish.
- Explain the thinking behind each direction - what it embodies, what
  it sacrifices.
- Push back if something in this brief is contradictory or unclear. The
  best outcome comes from genuine dialogue, not compliance.
